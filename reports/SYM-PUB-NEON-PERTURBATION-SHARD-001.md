## S — Signal

The source is important because it surfaces an immediately testable MCORE-1 systems question: whether a deterministic transform remains equivalent when processed monolithically versus across shards with explicit boundary-state handoff, and whether local perturbations create measurable cross-boundary propagation.

**Importance rationale:**

- **Strategic leverage: high.** It turns the existing carry-cascade narrative into a bounded experimental program.
- **Execution leverage: high.** The first useful artifact is a small correctness harness, not a speculative platform.
- **Evidence strength: low to moderate.** The source is an interdisciplinary synthesis, not a demonstrated MCORE-1 result.
- **Narrative risk: high.** Its Neon, AI-interpretability, and distributed-systems connections can overstate what has actually been shown.

## P — Proof

- **Receipt:** *Neon • Perturbation Theory • Database Sharding — Meta-Analysis Report* (June 2026), especially its executive summary and Sections 3–4.
  - **Statement:** The report identifies perturbation theory as a bridge between controlled deviation and system evolution, and sharding as a pattern for partitioned, fault-isolated computation.
  - **Method / retrieval context:** Source review and claim-tier audit.
  - **Result:** These are legitimate conceptual inputs for an experiment about partition invariance and perturbation propagation.
  - **Claim tier:** `established` for the general perturbation and sharding foundations.

- **Receipt:** Source report, Section 4.1, “Perturbation ↔ Shard.”
  - **Statement:** Local failures, partitions, hotspots, and boundary effects are suitable objects for controlled perturbation analysis in a sharded state system.
  - **Method / retrieval context:** Translation from the report’s systems framing to an MCORE-1 testable proposition.
  - **Result:** The strongest transferable idea is the **Perturbation × Shard** dyad.
  - **Claim tier:** `plausible` until tested against a specified MCORE-1 state model.

- **Receipt:** Source report, Sections 4.2–4.3.
  - **Statement:** The report explicitly acknowledges sparse direct cross-literature and proposes a sharded perturbation simulator / trit-shard state machine as a future artifact.

  - **Result:** The document is a routing artifact, not a conclusion artifact.

## L — Limit

- **Strongest counterpoint:** No result in the source demonstrates that MCORE-1 is partition-invariant, diagnostically superior, advantageous for distributed computing, or relevant to AI interpretability.
- **Known blind spots:**
  - The report does not provide a reproducible arXiv corpus method, screening criteria, or claim-to-paper map.
  - The report spans nuclear physics, quantum theory, databases, blockchains, and AI without an experiment that directly connects those domains.
  - “Sharding scales linearly” is workload- and coordination-dependent, not a universal systems fact.
  - Neon is currently a semantic/visual/observability motif for Symonic, not evidence that neon physics supports MCORE-1.
- **What this does not prove:**
  - That carry cascades predict shard-recomposition failure.
  - That an MCORE-derived metric exceeds Hamming distance, edit distance, hashes, or conventional integrity schemes.
  - That MCORE-1 reveals model reasoning, thought, AI safety failures, or physical neon phenomena.
